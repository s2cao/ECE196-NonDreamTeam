The Dangers of Drowsy Driving

Excessive Daytime Sleepiness (EDS) is a disorder that affects 20% of the global population. While this may seem like at most an inconvenience in daily life, there are certain aspects that those without this disorder take for granted. When EDS afflicted people are driving, it's common for them to dose off during long periods of monotany, such as a long highway. This results in a lack of focus and dangerous driving.



The HypnoGlove

The HypnoGlove: A smart glove that can recognize when you're falling asleep and alert you in various ways to keep you focused on the road.

This glove uses R-R Interval Detection and Sampling to detect abnormalities in Heartrate and recognize drowsiness in the user.



Flowchart of the HypnoGlove state

The Hypnoglove is our solution to driving with EDS. This glove can detect when you are drifting to sleep by measuring fluctuations and abnormailities in the R-R intervals of your heartbeat. When it detects these factors related to drowsiness, it will provide stimuli in the form of blinking lights and vibration to wake the driver.



Left: PCB Schematic
Right: PCB 3D View

When designing the HypnoGlove PCB, we wanted to keep in mind several factors. The important factors included current draw for our external components and size to fit in our glove. We decided to use Bipolar Junction Transistors (See Workshop) to amplify the current draw from our external battery to our LED Strip and Vibration Motor. We designed the PCB to be roughly a square that could fit on the back of an average hand.

Graph of time between detected R-R intervals

We soldered several components including resistors, transistors and capacitors to our PCB, along with connections to our external components like the LED Strip and Vibration motor.

After successfully soldering PCB components, we soldered connections to our Lithium-Polymer battery. Initially, the connection solely only wires, but we added a switch to limit power drain when not in use (Thanks Karcher).

When designing the ECG system, we initially used an Arduino MEGA to obtain readings on the serial monitor, and transitioned to our second Seeed Xiao Microcontroller in our final iteration.
The heartbeat readings had to be filtered using various filters and functions to obtain a less noisy signal and identifiable R-R peaks. These peaks would later be used to help recognize abnormalities between the user's regular heartrate and times when the user starts to doze off and fall asleep.
